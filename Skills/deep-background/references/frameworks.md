# Evidence and Argument Frameworks

Read this file before producing a fact-check analysis. It is the reference for classifying and rating evidence, running a Toulmin analysis, weighing sources, resolving contradictions, handling stated motives, and labelling the structure of disagreement when "reading the room."

Adapted from Mike Caulfield's Deep Background / SIFT Toolbox prompt (openly published for reuse): https://checkplease.neocities.org/sift-toolbox/index.html

## Evidence Types and Backing

Classify every piece of evidence by what *kind* of backing supports it, then ask that type's credibility question. Backing does not have to be strong to be classified — the point is to name what is being used to support a claim, then judge whether it holds.

| Evidence Type | Credibility Source | Common Artifacts | Credibility Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| Documentation | Direct artifacts | Photos, emails, video | Is this real and unaltered? |
| Personal Testimony | Direct experience | Witness accounts, statements, friend-of-a-friend reports | Was this person there? Are they a reliable witness? |
| Statistics | Appropriateness of method and representativeness | Charts, ratios, maps | Are these statistics accurate? Is the method sound and the sample representative? |
| Analysis | Expertise of the speaker | Research, statements to press | Does this person have relevant expertise? Do they have a history of being careful with the truth? |
| Reporting | Professional method that ascertains accounts, verifies evidence, solicits expertise | News reporting | Does this source abide by professional standards? Do they have verification expertise? |
| Common Knowledge | Existing agreement | Bare reference | Is this something we already agree on? |

When discussing evidence backing, always:

1. Identify the type of backing (e.g. "Documentation", "Personal Testimony").
2. Place the backing type in parentheses after discussing the evidence.
3. Address the relevant credibility question for that type.
4. Remember that weak backing still gets classified — you are categorizing what supports the claim, not pre-judging it.

### Linguistic analysis

Examine key phrases for loaded terms that smuggle in assumptions:

* Look for **totalizing language** ("everything", "all", "never", "always").
* Identify **causative claims** that assume a direct relationship where only correlation or sequence is shown.
* Note **emotional or evaluative terms** that pre-load a judgment.
* In your own writing, avoid "commonly presented" and write "presented" or "has been presented" — *unless* you have two or more citations showing something is in fact commonly or widely presented.

## Toulmin Analysis Framework

When analyzing claims, work through these steps:

1. Identify the core claim(s): what is the bigger point being made?
2. Uncover unstated assumptions and warrants (the implicit "this counts as evidence because…").
3. Evaluate the backing evidence using the Evidence Types table above.
4. Consider potential rebuttals.
5. Weigh counter-evidence.
6. Assess strengths and weaknesses.
7. Formulate a detailed verdict.

The weighting of these steps can be tuned to the topic; this is a first pass, not appropriate for every task.

## Evidence Evaluation Criteria (1–5)

Evidence evaluation determines the *mix* of sources to surface, not a verdict on quality. A high score means "a person investigating this issue will want to see this." A good result set has a lot of material at the top of the scale and some at the bottom — low-rated sources are still worth surfacing to characterize the surrounding discourse.

* **Documentary evidence (5)**: original primary-source documents, official records.
* **Photographic evidence (4–5)**: period photographs with clear provenance.
* **Contemporary accounts (4)**: news reports and journals from the relevant time.
* **Expert analysis (3–4)**: scholarly research, academic publications.
* **Second-hand accounts (2–3)**: later interviews, memoirs, biographies.
* **Social media / forums (1–2)**: uncorroborated online discussion — poor for factual backing, but excellent for showing what the surrounding discourse looks like.

## Source Usefulness Treatment

1. **Wikipedia**: a starting point (3–4); verify with primary sources.
2. **News outlets**: rate on reputation, methodology, and sources cited (2–5).
3. **Social media**: high skepticism unless claims are verified or the source is a known expert (1–2); still useful to characterize discourse.
4. **Academic sources**: generally reliable but still require verification and context (4–5).
5. **Primary documents**: highest usefulness (5), but context matters and provenance/authorship should be foregrounded when presenting.

State-controlled media (controlled, not merely funded) gets an asterisk in the sources table and the note: *State-controlled media, not a reliable source on anything that intersects with its national interests or the interest of the ruling party.*

## Handling Contradictions

When sources contradict each other:

1. Prioritize primary sources over secondary where the meaning is clear.
2. Consider temporal proximity — surface and summarize sources closest to the event.
3. Evaluate the potential biases or limitations of each source.
4. Acknowledge the contradiction explicitly in the assessment rather than papering over it.
5. If the evidence is inconclusive, default to the most well-supported position overall, and say that is what you are doing.

## The "stated motives" caution

People are experts on their own motives, but they do not always tell the whole truth. They often give rational-sounding reasons for actions actually driven by self-interest, animus, or convenience. For a stated motivation to be fully believed, it must be consistent with the person's history and behaviour — not just with their statements.

## Disagreement structure ("read the room")

When asked to "read the room," summarize the structure of expert and public opinion using these categories *explicitly* and by name. The distinction between minority and fringe is the one most often gotten wrong, so apply it carefully.

* **Competing theories**: multiple explanations exist; most experts back one or another, but no single idea is dominant.
* **Majority / minority**: one widely accepted theory, but a nontrivial number of respected experts back one or more alternatives that the majority concedes are worth considering.
* **Consensus**: a rare state where most experts find the evidence so compelling that the question is effectively closed. A few may pursue alternatives at the margins, but the discipline has largely moved on.
* **Uncertainty**: looks like majority/minority or competing theories at first, but on closer inspection most experts are so uncertain they have not invested deeply in any one hypothesis (the "we just don't know" situation).
* **Fringe**: viewpoints with no support among the vast majority of respected scholars or professionals. Crucially, fringe views are *not* minority views — minority views are in dialogue with the majority and argued back and forth; fringe views are not in dialogue with the field at all, because they have not engaged the existing conversation or body of knowledge.
