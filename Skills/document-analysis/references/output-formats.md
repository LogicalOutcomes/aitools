# Structured Output Formats

Match the output format to the user's question; offer alternatives when the best format is
unclear. These are working-notes formats — they inform the analyst's own writing, they are
not deliverable text. Fill-in skeletons for the structured formats are in
`assets/output-templates.md`; use them when producing an output as a file.

Universal requirements across all formats:

- Participant codes (P1, P2) or role descriptors instead of names, always.
- Every claim cites a location (page, section, table, or transcript line).
- States / implies / infers labels on substantive claims.
- Limitations noted before findings, not after.

## Document Summary

For single-document orientation. Covers: key findings, methodology, conclusions, limitations,
and document structure. Keep it concise — a summary the user has to summarize has failed.

## Thematic Analysis

For interview transcripts or other qualitative data. For each theme: theme statement;
supporting quotes with locations; prominence (verified count or descriptive frequency
language — see `references/qualitative-analysis.md`); counter-evidence or exceptions; and
cross-document consistency when multiple sources are involved. **Always marked as a draft for
analyst review**, with the qualitative transparency note and the text-as-written limitations
note included.

## Gap Analysis

For identifying weaknesses or missing pieces. Covers: what the document addresses; what it
claims to cover but doesn't adequately address; what is ambiguous; questions raised by the
evidence that the document doesn't answer; and what needs follow-up. Distinguish "absent from
the document" from "inadequately addressed" — they call for different follow-ups.

## Cross-Document Comparison

For multiple related documents. Covers: areas of agreement; areas of disagreement or
contradiction; complementary findings that together tell a more complete story; and gaps no
document addresses. Identify each document consistently (short labels defined up front) so
every claim is traceable to its source.

## Evidence Map

For quality review of reports — does the report's own evidence justify its own conclusions?
A claims-by-evidence table: each claim made in the document; the evidence presented for it
(with location); the strength of that evidence (well-supported / weakly supported /
unsupported, with a one-line reason); and gaps where claims lack support. The assessment is
internal-logic only, not external fact-checking — unless the user asks for external
verification, in which case keep the two clearly separated.

## Coding Summary

For qualitative data: proposed codes, definitions, example quotes with locations, and
frequencies (verified counts computed programmatically where they matter; otherwise
descriptive language). **Always marked as draft for analyst review**, with the transparency
note. A coding summary proposes a vocabulary for the analyst's codebook — it is the start of
coding, not the result of it.
