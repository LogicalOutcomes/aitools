## How to use this document

Evaluations of community and social service programs often involve common steps. This guide helps make the evaluation process quicker and cheaper by starting with these steps and adding more specific parts only if needed. It covers everything you need for an effective evaluation of small- to medium-sized programs, with a focus on improving services and listening to the people and groups involved. If you're looking for something more detailed, you can add additional methods as described in the [Appendix](#appendix).

The guide is written from the perspective of an evaluator or organization, to make it easier to paste content directly into your evaluation plan or proposal. Adapt it to fit your program's vocabulary, for example by replacing "participants" with "clients," "students," "learners," "patients," or "service users."

Evaluation projects are a chance for staff and decision makers to reflect on what they are doing and to improve. This evaluation approach aims to maximize the impact of that process. It incorporates learnings gleaned over many decades working with community service agencies and other nonprofit organizations in Ontario and beyond.

## Evaluation objectives

The first step in the evaluation process is to identify why you are doing it. There are three main reasons why we evaluate:

1. **A funder requires it.**
2. **Managers or Board members** are concerned about specific issues and want to delve into them.
3. **Program staff** want to improve their services to participants or the community.

It is possible to combine all three in a single evaluation, but you need to know what is really driving the decision to evaluate because that will determine your methodology.

No matter why you do it, a well-designed evaluation will result in an increased capacity to:

- Meet participant needs and help participants achieve their goals.
- Achieve program goals and outcomes.
- Improve program quality and fidelity.
- Increase responsiveness to participants in the way services are provided.
- Increase accessibility and equity in service provision (as defined by the program itself; e.g., for persons with disabilities, people living on low incomes, people from defined racial or ethnic groups, for underserved groups in the target population).
- Increase responsiveness to key stakeholders (as defined by the program; e.g., to the broader community served, employees, volunteers, funders, donors).

The basic evaluation framework below will meet each of those objectives to some degree. Additional objectives may require other methods — for a list of examples, see the [Appendix](#additional-evaluation-objectives-and-methods).

## Evaluation questions

Evaluation questions are grounded in your priorities as defined above and should determine the methods you use. For example, if evaluation is driven by:

**A funder:** Tie your methods to the funder's questions, minimizing the burden on staff and participants as much as possible. Demonstrate that the funding is well-spent and delivers benefits, accentuating the positive while responding honestly. At the same time, use the evaluation resources to improve your services since you are collecting information anyway.

**Managers or Board members:** Tie your methods to the specific questions, e.g., financial cost-benefit analysis, human resource processes, organizational culture, community conflict with the agency, future funding risks and so on. Include stakeholder engagement methods to ensure that issues are correctly understood and addressed.

**Program staff:** Focus on participant feedback, process analysis and team problem-solving methods, and assess how information is actually being used in service improvements. Evaluation for service improvement is like a prolonged diagnostic process in which you begin with identifying pain points and then systematically collect information that helps you decide on and test solutions.

### Core evaluation questions

At the very least, your evaluation questions should include the following:

1. What activities are provided by the program?
2. What do participants think about services and what are their suggestions?
3. To what extent are participants meeting their goals?
4. What do staff and other key stakeholders think about services and what are their suggestions?
5. To what extent is the program meeting quality standards?

### Additional questions (if using a CMS)

If your program uses a Client Management System (CMS) that tracks demographic and service information about individual participants, you should also be able to answer the following:

6. What are the characteristics of the populations served by the program?
7. To what extent does participation vary by population group?
8. What is the average and median number of sessions attended by participants?
9. What is the program's overall completion rate, and how do completion rates vary by population group and across program sites?
10. To what extent is participation in the program associated with improved short-term outcomes among all participants and among different population groups?

> **Note:** If your program is not using a CMS, these questions will be harder to answer. You would need to set up segmented sampling and provide incentives to staff and possibly participants to get a reasonable response rate.

## Evaluation phases and activities

This evaluation methodology emphasizes continuous program feedback and improvement throughout the evaluation project, compressing the typical time for initial planning and design. The intent is to focus evaluation resources as much as possible on understanding and responding to stakeholder perspectives and on improving program quality.

The evaluation project will carry out the following activities in four overlapping phases:

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|:------|:---------|:---------------|
| **1. Planning & Initiation** | 3–8 weeks | Engage the project sponsor, team members and key decision-makers · Define evaluation objectives and questions · Define program or service activities · Define stakeholders and their interests, goals and concerns |
| **2. Design & Development** | 1–12 weeks | Define desired program outcomes and risks from perspectives of key stakeholders · Select measures for program activities and participant goals/milestones · Select measures for program outcomes and stakeholder feedback · Develop, refine and test data collection tools · Collect data |
| **3. Implementation & Analysis** | Ongoing | Design reports and visualizations for monthly, quarterly and final reports |
| **4. Communication & Discussion** | 3–6 weeks | Produce and discuss reports · Communicate and disseminate results and recommendations |

The use of standard data collection tools whenever possible will compress the timeline for the first two phases (i.e., closer to a total of four weeks[^1] instead of 20 weeks). It is impossible to get perfect data collection tools on the first try with any organization or community, and the attempt will delay evaluation and exhaust stakeholders. Instead, we will start with "good enough" versions that have worked for other communities and then revise them for future evaluation projects based on user feedback.

[^1]: The first two phases could be achieved with two or three intensive structured 3-hour workshops involving key people over the period of two or three weeks after project kick-off. See [Agenda for planning workshops](#roles-and-meeting-agendas) in Appendix.

### Phase 1: Planning and Initiation

**1. Engage the project sponsor, team members and key decision-makers.**

- Define the main roles and responsibilities for the evaluation project, including the manager who is overseeing the project and who will be taking the recommendations to senior management.
- Define the timelines, resources (including staff effort for interviews and data collection) and the decision process for approval of final report.

**2. Define evaluation objectives and questions.**

- Using the generic objectives and evaluation questions as a basis for discussion, agree on what the evaluation aims to achieve.
- Review additional objectives and methodologies if appropriate and decide whether to add them to the scope of the project.

**3. Define program or service activities.**

- Define the specific activities that are delivered by the program and that are essential to the program. The basic evaluation framework in this document focuses mainly on direct services to participants, but most programs also include other elements like collaboration with other organizations, employee training, financial management and so on.
- This step aims to narrow down to the activities that must be included in order to meet the evaluation's objectives.

**4. Define stakeholders and their interests, goals and concerns.**

- Stakeholders for the program probably include participants, staff, partner organizations, the broader community, funders and donors. Some programs target local school boards, employers, universities and specific community groups.
- The evaluation team should define key stakeholders or groups that should be engaged in data collection and/or discussion of the results, and briefly capture possible goals and concerns regarding the program.
- Note that a full stakeholder analysis would require an additional module to the project (see [Appendix](#additional-evaluation-objectives-and-methods)).

### Phase 2: Design and Development

**5. Define desired program outcomes and risks from perspectives of key stakeholders.**

- In this step, the project team will summarize the program outcomes that have been identified in the previous steps, incorporating key stakeholder goals as well as the official outcomes defined by the program model and funders.
- Major risks will also be identified, including the potential harms that the program may cause. These goals and risks should be addressed in the implementation of the evaluation.

**6. Select measures for program activities and participant goals and milestones.**

- The Client Management System will be reviewed to assess whether it can generate reports for participant goals, milestones and activities.
- Every participant should be asked what they hope to get from the services, and if feasible asked later if they achieved their goals. If necessary, the CMS will be revised to include those questions (see the Appendix for samples).

**7. Select measures for program outcomes and stakeholder feedback.**

- The project team will select interview and/or survey questions from a list of standard data collection tools.
- Short-term outcome measures will be derived from an analysis of the Client Management System, supplemented by interviews with small samples of participants.

**8. Develop, refine and test data collection tools.**

- Once measures have been selected, they may need to be adapted and customized for the program or community. This step may take several months, especially if significant adaptations require pilot testing and translation.
- As much as possible, the evaluation will use standard data collection tools and then collect user feedback throughout the project so that the tools can be improved for subsequent evaluations.
- Exports from an existing CMS may require anonymization or asking for reports from the organization's internal I.T. department to protect confidentiality.

### Phase 3: Implementation and Analysis

**9. Design reports and visualizations for monthly, quarterly and final reports.**

- The project team will select the reporting formats that will be used to share results throughout the period of the evaluation project.
- Data reports and visualizations should be designed as early as possible so that emerging findings can be shared with stakeholders.
- Standard data collection tools come with report designs that can be used immediately, including visualizations of goal achievement and qualitative results from interviews.

**10. Collect data.**

- The project team will oversee data collection, ensuring that data is being collected correctly and is of high quality.
- Frequent interim reports will assess whether the data appears to be accurate and whether it captures feedback from defined stakeholders and demographic groups.
- If necessary, additional demographic segments may be targeted with additional interviews or financial incentives.

### Phase 4: Communication and Discussion

**11. Produce and discuss reports.**

- Reports will be delivered frequently and discussed with appropriate staff and stakeholders with a focus on recommended actions. Emerging findings may be tested with follow-up interviews or more detailed reports.
- Notes from staff meetings will be reviewed for evidence of actions that have been taken or recommendations that have been made as a result of the reports.

**12. Communicate and disseminate results and recommendations.**

- Finally, the results and recommendations generated by the evaluation will be disseminated to a broader audience or to the key decision-makers who should be implementing changes.

## Basic evaluation framework

The following evaluation framework includes all the elements and measures needed for most one- or two-year evaluations of community and social services. An evaluation is an intervention in itself and has its own logic model, which means that a generic evaluation framework can include many standard data collection tools.

The framework works best if the agency is using some sort of Client Management System (CMS) to track individual participants and their progress. If the agency does not have a CMS, it can use staff interviews and document reviews to estimate many of the answers. The results will be less detailed but may still deliver useful and credible inputs for decision-making. Other than the CMS recommendation, this framework uses methods that are quick and simple to implement, impose a minimum burden on staff and participants, engage staff in problem identification and solving, and build capacity for service improvement.

The basic framework does not include direct measures of long-term outcomes since they are rarely measured directly as part of evaluation projects. Neither does the basic framework include measures for costs and inputs. These measures may be added depending on the evaluation objectives — see the [Appendix](#additional-evaluation-objectives-and-methods).

> Every evaluation should incorporate measures for program quality and participant feedback. Outcome surveys should be used only if the sampling method provides statistical validity or if the survey results are actually used to improve services. Evaluations should collect data that can be used to improve service delivery, and all data should be critically assessed to ensure that it is credible to stakeholders and used in decision-making.

### Outputs (reported quarterly)

| Logic Model Element | Measure | Respondent | Data Collection | Comments |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Participants served | # participants | Project manager | CMS analysis or staff interviews | If possible, include demographic breakdown compared with targets linked to equity and inclusion objectives |
| Services provided | # encounters · # episodes | Project manager | CMS analysis or staff interviews | Includes type of activity, encounters, episodes. Compare with target level of service delivery |
| Delivery milestones for evaluation | # weeks +/- target · Quality rating | Project manager | Document review of progress reports · Thematic analysis of optional "description" field · Observation or audit | Delivery dates for evaluation plan, data collection, clean data, progress and final reports etc. compared to target dates. Include quality rating, e.g., evaluation plan has framework approved by sponsor; data is clean and useful; consent obtained; minimum risk of harm; quality is good enough for purpose |

### Short-term outcomes (reported quarterly)

| Logic Model Element | Measure | Respondent | Data Collection | Comments |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Meet participant needs | Goal questionnaire | Participant | Questionnaire or interview | Collected in CMS or with attached survey |
| Increase responsiveness to participants | Suggestions · Participant satisfaction | Participant | Questionnaire or interview | Multiple languages including audio; can cautiously include demographics and stakeholder type. Minimize "client satisfaction" surveys unless incorporated into service delivery |
| Improve program quality & fidelity | Impact interview · # team debriefs · Thematic analysis | Reviewer[^2] | Observation or audit | Team debriefs are based on notes from team meetings in which the agenda includes program quality and feedback from participants |

Minimize "satisfaction" surveys unless incorporated into service changes |

[^2]: Review may be by an external or internal evaluator, project manager, or another manager, staff member or volunteer assigned to the project.

| Logic Model Element | Measure | Comments |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Achieve program outcomes | Aggregated mid-term program outcomes or separate long-term evaluation project | Include participant outcomes as well as any other long-term program outcomes. Eventually via CMS |

| Improve organizational sustainability | Separate evaluation project | Includes financial, human resources as well as governance and management processes |

## Data collection tools

The primary data collection tools will be a CMS database (new or existing), supplemented by interviews with participants and other stakeholders. For a list of standard data collection tools see the [Appendix](#standard-data-collection-tools). Several of them have been tested and refined with hundreds of participants and translated into over a dozen languages.

### Outcome measures

Short-term outcomes can be collected as part of the CMS. Questions about participant goals, goal achievement, demographics, satisfaction and program outcomes should be added if they are not already included. Outcome measures should be tightly tied to the program goals and be useful in working with individual participants. For example, language training programs might do language assessments and employment programs might track employment-related skills or job interviews.

In some cases, organizations may want additional surveys collected outside the CMS, with measures such as "life satisfaction," "stress," and the like. **We recommend that the use of surveys be minimized.** In most cases they are neither helpful nor necessary for a good evaluation, not least because the response rate is usually low and the results statistically invalid. Surveys should be collected only if the results are used to improve service provision and/or sampling is done in a way that is generalizable. Further, they should be as brief as possible to reduce the burden on participants and staff.

As an example of questions that might be used in service delivery, consider the **PRAPARE screening tool** (Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients' Assets, Risks, and Experiences). It was developed for U.S. community health centres to promote the inclusion of social drivers of health in assessments and treatment plans and has been translated into over 25 languages. The measures most relevant to community and social services are *social and emotional health*:

> **Social connection:** How often do you see or talk to people that you care about and feel close to (for example: talking to friends on the phone, visiting friends or family, going to church or club meetings)?
> *Less than once a week · 1 or 2 times a week · 3 to 5 times a week · 6 or more times a week · I choose not to answer this question*
>
> **Stress:** Stress is when someone feels tense, nervous, anxious or can't sleep at night because their mind is troubled — how stressed are you?
> *Not at all · A little bit · Somewhat · Quite a bit · Very much · I choose not to answer this question*

We suggest that if surveys are necessary, they use standardized questions sourced from valid and credible instruments. We recommend the **Canadian Community Health Survey** (CCHS) and other Statistics Canada surveys which address most aspects of life, health, work, education and social connections. Specifically, check out the **Quality of Life Framework for Canada** (Government of Canada, 2022). Also see Canada's **Positive Mental Health Surveillance Indicator Framework** (Government of Canada, 2023). Both frameworks include "sense of belonging" and "life satisfaction" as key indicators.

### Process metrics for implementation fidelity and program quality

It is important to define the key elements of the program within the CMS to ensure that participants are getting the right services without falling through the cracks. Most programs involve several steps: initial intake and assessment, service delivery and follow-up. Some organizations provide additional steps such as job placement or referrals. Process measures can get bogged down in discussions of fine-grained workflows, which prolong evaluations unnecessarily. Metrics should focus on the absolute requirements of the program and identify priorities for improvement. Subsequent evaluations can add more complex workflows as the organization's capacity increases.

Depending on the program, basic metrics might include:

- **Guidelines compliance:** The consistent use of up-to-date guidelines for intake and assessment, service delivery and record keeping consistent with the program design and organizational policy. This includes staff supervision to ensure that new staff are familiar with and actually use the guidelines defined by the agency.
- **Timely intake:** Within a set time of first contact, the vast majority of referrals (e.g., 80%) including self-referrals will have completed initial intake or eligibility screening and either be accepted into the program, referred elsewhere or turned down.
- **Completion rate:** Most enrolled participants (e.g., 80%) will successfully complete the program or course.
- **Use of feedback:** Demonstrated use of participant feedback in service delivery over the period of a year (e.g., as evidenced in notes of program changes). It is not enough to show that participant feedback is being collected; the organization must be able to show evidence that the feedback has been used to improve services.

> If organizations define clear service standards (e.g., 80% of participants will successfully complete the course), they can troubleshoot if metrics fall below the service standard.

## Appendix

### Additional evaluation objectives and methods

The basic evaluation framework addresses a broad range of questions using a minimalistic set of generic tools. In many cases, the evaluation will come up with suggestive but not conclusive findings that need further digging before the organization makes major changes. The organization can add methodologies to test hypotheses or make a stronger case for investment.

| # | Objective | Suggested Methods |
|:--|:----------|:------------------|
| 1 | **Improve program design** based on research or empirical evidence | Add a literature review based on high quality research papers and possibly interviews with qualified experts in the field |
| 2 | **Increase implementation fidelity** (program fidelity) | Compare activities actually provided with the program design as intended. Add specific implementation fidelity measures tied to the program design |
| 3 | **Increase employee engagement** | Investigate team performance, engagement, job satisfaction and workplace environment including incidents of harassment and misconduct |
| 4 | **Increase equity and diversity** | Use equity audits, community-based participatory research and other DEI approaches to investigate equity at various levels of the organization — what services are provided to whom, how participants are treated, who gets hired and promoted, who makes the major decisions and how those decisions are made |
| 5 | **Improve management efficiency and processes** | Add business process analysis, cost effectiveness and cost/benefit analysis. Suitable lightweight methods include participant journey mapping and "Five Whys" based on staff interviews |
| 6 | **Assess organizational strategy and sustainability** | Add strategic planning and operational planning tools like SWOT analysis |
| 7 | **Perform an environmental scan and stakeholder analysis** | Use scenario planning, trend analysis, stakeholder mapping and network analysis to investigate the organization's external environment for opportunities, risks, trends and challenges |
| 8 | **Improve the rationale and/or marketing message** for funding and resources | Add donor research, market analysis, benchmarking against competitors, message testing and other marketing techniques |
| 9 | **Assess long-term impacts** of the program | Use a rigorous research design with control groups and long-term data collection or an in-depth analysis of large external longitudinal datasets |
| 10 | **Compare efficacy against other programs** | Use methods that ensure fair comparisons: statistically robust sampling, high data quality and case mix adjustments. Also use common taxonomies and data specifications such as the Common Impact Data Standard, the Data Documentation Initiative and Athena |

### Standard data collection tools

Many of the following tools have been tested with hundreds of participants in different languages and can be implemented immediately.

- **CMS fields** for participant goals, goal achievement, demographics and satisfaction (new or existing)
- **Brief suggestion poll** for participants and other stakeholders with optional fields for satisfaction, stakeholder type, demographics and service type (fields may be hidden as part of the survey link, accompanied with the appropriate consent agreement)
- **Interview protocols** for participants and other key stakeholders with fields for stakeholder type, program and (optionally) demographics
- **Program quality checklist**
- **Process metrics**
- **Qualitative notes of process improvement meetings** — the standard agenda should include review of participant comments and process metrics, actions to be taken as a result of participant comments and process issues, and follow-ups on previous actions
- **Rating scale for organizational implementation plans**, measuring specificity and responsiveness to the evaluation results (to be administered during the final activity of the project)
- **Milestones for the evaluation project itself**, including target date, date achieved and comments:
  - Project charter or evaluation plan approved by Sponsor
  - Data collection tools launched
  - Data aggregated, cleaned and prepared for analysis (for each data collection tool)
  - Interim reports (e.g., quarterly and/or annual summarizing progress of evaluation and showing emerging findings)
  - Final report (submitted and reviewed by senior management)
- **Statistics Canada survey questions** that may be used as potential outcome measures (CCHS etc.). The questions have been tested in English and French, are often based on validated instruments, and can be benchmarked against other communities. By definition, given the massive public financial investment in Statistics Canada surveys, every question has demonstrated relevance to at least some decision-makers.

### Roles and meeting agendas

The main purpose of this document is to reduce the burden of evaluation while maximizing its useful information. That includes minimizing unnecessary staff time in the evaluation process. There are only a few times when senior managers and decision-makers must get involved:

1. Defining objectives, roles and key stakeholders at the beginning of the evaluation.
2. Engaging with data as soon as it starts to be collected and then occasionally throughout the project.
3. Engaging with the final recommendations and action plans near the end of the evaluation.

In addition, representatives of the organization need to be involved more deeply in:

4. Defining the program services and key processes.
5. Defining project timelines and tracking quality.
6. Configuring the CMS and refining the data collection tools.

#### Essential roles

There are four essential roles for a successful evaluation project:

**Sponsor** (generally a senior manager who will be responsible for implementing recommendations)

- Provide guidance regarding overall objectives and constraints of project
- Liaise with the organization's senior management and manage organizational expectations and scope issues as appropriate
- Communicate with internal and external stakeholders regarding project progress
- Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Liaison
- Approve significant changes to the project scope, timeline, budget, or quality if required
- Review and approve project documents and other deliverables

**Liaison** (generally a manager at the organization reporting to the Project Sponsor)

- Act as the primary contact person with the evaluator
- Liaise with the Project Sponsor and take on their responsibilities as delegated
- Act as a project manager from the organization's side, e.g., scheduling meetings with program staff, negotiating with I.T. staff

**Project Owner** (generally a senior external evaluator)

- Provide project leadership for the evaluation as a whole
- Define project methodologies and advise on strategic issues
- Author, review and approve project documents as assigned
- Manage and resolve team-level risks, issues, and changes
- Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Project Sponsor
- Review and provide detailed feedback regarding all project documents and deliverables

**Project Manager** (generally an external evaluator reporting to the Project Owner)

- Act as liaison to the organization for operational issues
- Monitor project scope, quality, schedule, resources, costs and risks
- Coordinate implementation of project work
- Ensure project plan, schedule, and budget are up-to-date; detect and manage discrepancies
- Report risks, delays and problems to Project Owner and Project Sponsor as they arise
- Author, review and approve project documents as assigned to ensure quality standards are met
- Arrange and follow-up on team meetings
- Manage and contribute to data collection, analysis and report writing
- Provide leadership and manage work as appropriate

**Program staff** (one or more representatives of the people who will actually implement service improvements and experience changes in their workplace)

Other common roles include I.T. specialists, program participants and representatives from key constituencies.

#### Meeting schedule

At a bare minimum, not including meetings that involve only the Project Liaison and Project Manager, the project should have four structured meetings with organizational representatives, e.g., by holding a full-day workshop to combine steps 2 and 3.

| Meeting | Deliverables | Minimum Attendees | Notes |
|:--------|:-------------|:------------------|:------|
| **1. Initiate the project** | Draft project charter | Liaison, Project Manager | The agenda for this meeting is the Project Charter template. The PM and Liaison fill out most of the project charter with input from Statement of Work, proposal and program documents |
| **2. Define objectives, roles and key stakeholders** | Project charter | Project Owner, Sponsor, Liaison, Program staff, PM | This meeting involves the organization's decision-makers and program staff to build engagement in the evaluation, to decide on priorities, and to commit on how they will handle problems as they arise. The completed Project Charter is approved by the Sponsor shortly after the meeting |
| **3. Configure CMS and data collection tools** | CMS design and approved data collection tools | Liaison, Program staff, PM | Use existing tools as much as possible. If further discussion needed, offer additional meetings to subgroup |
| **4. Track progress and troubleshoot problems** | Progress report | Liaison, PM | This can be combined with other meetings. The purpose is to discuss delays and solve problems as they arise |
| **5. Engage with data** | Meeting notes with actions | Liaison, Program staff, PM (monthly at first, then bimonthly) | These meetings should be a recurring agenda item in regular program staff meetings, if feasible. Part of the purpose is to get staff accustomed to responding to participant feedback in their staff meetings as a normal reflective practice. Emerging conclusions should be addressed early and often so that staff get a chance to contribute to recommendations before they are presented to senior management |
| **6. Engage with recommendations** | Revised conclusions and recommendations | Project Owner, Sponsor, Liaison, Program staff, PM | This may require multiple meetings with different stakeholder groups, with revisions at each stage incorporating their feedback. Present only conclusions and recommendations to encourage discussion. Technical details should be available for questions and circulated to interested stakeholders |

### Simple client management system

LogicalOutcomes is developing a simple client management system that incorporates a lightweight, highly efficient approach to evaluation. It is intended to build organizational capacity to serve individual participants while generating useful evaluation reports for the program as a whole.

This new CMS is based on experience with hundreds of evaluations, but most specifically on our insights from the inspirational projects described below. We realized that a useful CMS can provide detailed longitudinal data about individual participants throughout the program life cycle, from intake to final follow-up. By including demographic information (with participant consent), organizations can track whether they are more or less successful with different groups.

At the same time, a CMS can manage many administrative requirements like collecting contact information, scheduling sessions, managing tasks and taking progress notes. And if the CMS is genuinely useful in service provision — by which we mean saving staff time while improving service quality — the response rate will be almost 100% with good data quality. The trick is to keep the CMS as simple as possible to minimize staff burden and add complexity over time as needed.

If designed correctly, such a CMS can also generate almost all of the data and reports that are necessary for good solid evaluations.

This document is essentially a design blueprint for the new CMS. A basic configuration should produce all the data and reports necessary for the evaluation framework. A more complex configuration can add process steps, questionnaires and surveys, and different kinds of data collection tools.

### Inspiration for this document

Three major initiatives at LogicalOutcomes inspired this work. All addressed limitations that are often responsible for unsatisfactory data collection in community service evaluations. All included budgets and resources for extended stakeholder consultations, the translation and testing of data collection tools, collaborative development of visualizations and reports, financial incentives to staff and participants, and ongoing discussions about findings with the organizations involved. The results of this massive effort include the standard data collection tools and templates in this document.

**Ontario Trillium Foundation Outcome Measurement Platform.** The first initiative was the Ontario Trillium Foundation's (OTF) Outcome Measurement Platform, which was provided by LogicalOutcomes from 2020 to 2024. The authors worked closely with the OTF evaluation team and hundreds of OTF-funded organizations to design data collection tools, processes and reports.

**Financial Empowerment Case Management Platform.** The second initiative was a multi-year project led by West Neighbourhood House and later Prosper Canada starting in 2020 to develop a case management platform for financial empowerment services. The program worked with graduates from skilled trade apprenticeship programs, self-employed informal economy workers, patients from a local hospital system, and clients of employment and settlement programs in an effort to improve their financial health and quality of life through financial coaching. As a direct result of our collaboration, LogicalOutcomes is developing a Client Management System that incorporates the evaluation framework in this document. Organizations will soon be able to use those CMS for both service provision and ongoing evaluation.

**Newcomer and Racialized Women's Services Evaluation.** The third initiative was a four-year evaluation (2019–2023) of a community research project regarding services for newcomer and racialized women experiencing domestic or intimate partner violence in the Greater Toronto area. The project was managed by COSTI Immigrant Services, funded by Women and Gender Equality Canada (WAGE), and involved five community-based agencies in the Greater Toronto Area (Dinshaw, In preparation).

#### Key findings from these projects

These inspirational projects confirmed our experience from hundreds of evaluations. Over the years, we have found that:

- There is limited staff time available for evaluation, and by the time the final report is delivered everyone is exhausted from the effort, with little energy left for actual implementation of changes.
- Evaluation effort should be focused on program improvements, with minimal attention paid to tweaking technical documentation like logic models.
- Organizations should build the capacity for future evaluations, testing and refining tools with each project, rather than starting from scratch every time.
- In most evaluations, the burden of collecting data in addition to regular client services is seen as low value by both staff and clients, and response rate is low.
- Most data collection tools are not integrated into client management processes and are tiresome stand-alone add-ons to an already challenging workplace. Digital tools are typically not supported by the organization's I.T. staff and require constant technical support and training to staff throughout the evaluation. Paper-based tools like printed surveys are cumbersome for other reasons.
- Evaluation itself is an intervention and has its own logic model. An effective evaluation should result in long-term improvements to organizations, programs and participants' lives. By treating evaluation as an intervention, it is possible to use generic tools and not re-do elements that have already been tested with similar organizations. That allows organizations to move quickly to collecting results and engaging staff in improvements.

#### Recommendations for evaluation methods

Our resulting conclusions about evaluation methods included these recommendations:

**1. Use participant data only to improve services**

This point is fundamental to evaluation and monitoring in general and underlies the rest of the recommendations. Participant data presents so many technical and ethical problems that it should be clearly useful, either to individual participants or to the program as a whole.

- There are large costs and barriers to data collection and reporting in community services. Data collection in particular presents a burden to both staff and participants, and unnecessary documentation has been shown to decrease staff morale and have other negative effects (Ommaya et al., 2018; Zegers et al., 2020).
- The benefits of data should be larger than the costs. If data is not being used to improve services, it should not be collected. This may require negotiations regarding funder-mandated surveys to improve their timeliness and usefulness.
- The costs of data collection, analysis and security should be adequately funded, including ongoing technical support and staff training.
- The results of data analysis must engage front-line staff as well as agency decision-makers. That means that reports should be relevant, frequent (e.g., monthly) and probably interactive so that staff can explore results, test hypotheses and see the impact of service changes. If front-line staff do not value the results of data collection, the data is unlikely to be accurate.

**2. Use participant-informed practice**

Feedback from participants should be continuously collected and used in service delivery. This approach prioritizes the empowerment and engagement of participants in the services they receive, and it recognizes that no program model, no matter how well designed and implemented, is ideal for every population.

Participant-informed and deliberate practice are related to lower drop-outs, higher client satisfaction, decreased negative impacts, greater counsellor effectiveness and higher impact in social services (Gondek et al., 2016; Graßmann et al., 2020; Miller et al., 2020; Swift & Greenberg, 2012). For managers, participant feedback gives them a view into service delivery and allows them to spot problems that should be addressed. Participant feedback can also provide proxies for outcomes, accountability, empowerment (Benitez et al., 2022) and thus can reduce the need for other types of measurement.

By definition, participant-informed practice requires engaging with ongoing feedback from participants to improve services. The steps include collecting feedback, reviewing it, discussing it with agency decision-makers, changing services in response to the feedback, and then going through the cycle again (Brattland et al., 2018).

Specific suggestions for using participant feedback include:

- Feedback can be collected in many different ways, including during sessions or informal conversations, not necessarily via structured surveys and focus groups.
- Feedback, however it is collected, should be reviewed regularly with staff (e.g., in monthly staff meetings), using a standard agenda that includes follow-up actions. Actions might include assessing the importance of issues by interviewing more participants or discussing service improvements with managers. The evaluation measure would be a summary of the actions deriving from the feedback, and could be collected from staff meeting notes (for example) or task lists.
- Open-ended qualitative responses seem to have the most impact on staff (in our experience), especially when the responses include appreciation for good work. When staff could see actual words of praise and recognition, they found it very rewarding and were more able to pay attention to suggestions and criticisms. Community work can be emotionally draining, and evaluation should minimize negative effects on staff while maximizing benefits to participants. In comparison to open-ended responses, aggregated scores and numbers from participant surveys are difficult to interpret and may not be compelling to staff.
- If using qualitative responses, organizations must protect the confidentiality of comments. Even without names, it is sometimes possible to guess the identity of a respondent. We recommend that informed consents on feedback tools include permission to quote excerpts anonymously since (in our experience) organizations will probably forget the wording of the consent form and include quotes in reports anyway.
- Where process or outcome metrics are not feasible (see next sections), organizations may interview a small sample of participants to assess service process and impact.

**3. Use process metrics to improve program quality**

Organizations should track a small number of key metrics that show whether the most important elements of service delivery are on track. This information should be embedded into the Client Management System as a regular part of service delivery.

We know from implementation research that program models are not useful in isolation (Fixsen et al., 2005). Without continual tracking and improvement to maintain program fidelity, programs tend to stray from their official models.

There are many ways to assess program fidelity (Carroll et al., 2007) but not all of them are useful in practice, especially for complex interventions involving people with multiple vulnerabilities (Hasson et al., 2012). In fact staff training, which is a type of fidelity measure, sometimes results in poorer actual fidelity (Muhammad et al., 2021). For the most effective approach to measurement, organizations should track a small number of key metrics that show whether the most important elements of service delivery are on track. This information should be embedded into the Client Management System as a regular part of service delivery. In cases where this data is not collected in their management systems, organizations may select a random or strategic sample of participants for process tracking.

It is important to define the key elements, sometimes called the "active ingredients," of the program model. For simple programs involving intake/assessment, service delivery and follow-up, basic metrics are described in the standard data collection tools above. More complex program models will have more detailed or complex workflows and metrics.

If organizations or funders define clear service standards (e.g., 80% of initial referrals to be contacted within 48 hours) they can troubleshoot if metrics fall below the service standard. For example, organizations might call participants back in the evenings, change the way they do follow-ups, work with referral partners to improve processes, make a case for additional funding, advocate publicly for better services, and so on.

However, implementing detailed process metrics requires a large investment in time and money, and does not necessarily result in improved services unless they are incorporated into regular management processes (De Vos et al., 2009; Institute for Healthcare Improvement, 2021; Sampath et al., 2021).

**4. Delay outcome measurement until a Client Management System is implemented**

Outcome measures should be integrated into a Client Management System so that they are incorporated into service delivery, do not require staff to switch I.T. platforms, and include demographic data for use in planning and service equity.

Until organizations have implemented a CMS, they should not invest in outcome measurement. A basic CMS should include outputs and short-term outcomes, including demographics and participant feedback, that can be used in service improvement as well as evaluations. Mid- and long-term outcome measures are generally not relevant since they are measured too late to influence a participant's services, are not seen as useful by staff, and are probably not statistically valid.

If a program is based on evidence and has been shown to work with a given population, the most appropriate use of measurement is to ensure that the services are being delivered correctly according to the program design, and that participants are able to influence the services they receive.
