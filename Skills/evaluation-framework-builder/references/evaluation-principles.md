# Evaluation Principles

This document explains *why* we evaluate the way we do at LogicalOutcomes. For *how* to do it, see the [Evaluation Process Guide](evaluation-services-guide.md).

---

## Two Core Principles

Everything in our approach flows from two foundational insights.

### Principle 1: Evaluation Is an Intervention

> **"Evaluation is a program intervention itself and has its own logic model."**

If evaluation is an intervention, then it should have positive effects—specifically, it should improve services. If your evaluation activities aren't leading to better programs, you shouldn't be doing them.

This is a practical position, not a philosophical one. Evaluation consumes staff time, participant attention, and organizational resources. The benefits should exceed the costs.

**What this means in practice:**

Every data point we collect must answer one question: *"What will we change based on this?"*

If the answer is "nothing" or "we don't know," don't collect it.

**Why this matters:**

Because evaluation is an intervention with its own logic model, we can use standardized tools across different programs. The evaluation structure stays consistent even when program content varies. This means:

- Organizations don't need to design evaluation from scratch for each program
- Staff learn one approach and can apply it across programs
- Data collection becomes routine rather than a special project

### Principle 2: Evaluation Is a Project

Evaluation doesn't happen automatically. It requires the same project management discipline as any other organizational initiative.

Without clear ownership and accountability, evaluation becomes nobody's priority—timelines slip, data quality suffers, and findings never lead to action.

**What this means in practice:**

- Someone must own the evaluation (strategic accountability)
- Someone must manage it (operational coordination)
- Milestones must be in the calendar
- Staff time must be budgeted

Evaluation that's nobody's job doesn't get done. Evaluation that's everybody's job doesn't get done either.

## Why We Prioritize Participant Tracker Data

We recommend participant tracker data (sometimes called CMS or case management data) as the primary data source over surveys or external data collection.

### The Practical Reason

Research on documentation in health and social services consistently shows that data collection seen as separate from service delivery becomes a burden that degrades both staff morale and data quality.

When data collection is integrated into service delivery—when staff enter information because it helps them work with participants—several things happen:

1. **Data is more complete.** Staff enter information as part of their regular workflow.
2. **Data is more accurate.** Staff care about accuracy because they're using the information.
3. **Data is more timely.** Information is captured during or immediately after service interactions.

### The Methodological Reason

A well-designed participant tracker provides longitudinal data about individual participants throughout the program lifecycle—from intake to final follow-up. By including demographic information (with consent), organizations can analyze whether they're more or less successful with different population groups.

This kind of analysis is difficult or impossible with periodic surveys.

## Why We Avoid Mass Surveys

We minimize surveys—particularly "client satisfaction surveys"—for three reasons:

### 1. Statistical Validity

Survey response rates in community services typically range from 10-30%. At these levels, results may not represent the participant population. People who respond to surveys differ systematically from those who don't—they may be more satisfied, more literate, have more time, or feel more connected to the program.

### 2. Actionability

For data to improve services, it must be credible to the people who deliver services. Survey results that arrive months after collection, summarized as aggregate scores, rarely lead to specific service changes. Staff may not trust results based on small samples of unknown participants.

### 3. Measurement Burden

Every survey adds to the documentation burden on both staff and participants. Unnecessary documentation decreases staff morale and has negative effects on organizational functioning.

### When Surveys Are Appropriate

Surveys can work when:

- **Sample sizes and methods support statistical validity** (requires dedicated resources)
- **Results are used in service delivery** (built into service encounters)
- **Standardized instruments are needed** for benchmarking
- **Small samples** (5-15 participants) provide qualitative insight without validity concerns

The framework doesn't prohibit surveys; it requires that any survey pass the core test: *What will we change based on this?*

## The Participant-Informed Practice Cycle

This is what makes evaluation an intervention that improves services:

```text
1. Collect feedback (during service delivery)
         ↓
2. Review in monthly staff meetings
         ↓
3. Discuss with decision-makers
         ↓
4. Change services in response
         ↓
5. Document changes → return to step 1
```

**Why each step matters:**

1. **During service delivery:** Feedback collected in the moment is richer than retrospective surveys.
2. **In staff meetings:** When staff discuss participant feedback themselves, they're more likely to trust and act on it.
3. **With decision-makers:** Some changes require management approval or resources.
4. **Service changes:** The point of the cycle. If nothing changes, it isn't working.
5. **Documentation:** Recording what changed creates evidence that evaluation matters.

### The Role of Qualitative Feedback

In our experience across hundreds of evaluations, open-ended qualitative responses have the most impact on staff—particularly when responses include appreciation for good work.

When staff can read actual words from participants, they find it rewarding and are more able to engage with suggestions and criticisms. Community work can be emotionally draining; evaluation should minimize negative effects on staff while maximizing benefits to participants.

## Research Base

The LogicalOutcomes framework is supported by research on systematic feedback in human services:

**Gondek et al. (2016)** conducted a systematic review finding that feedback from outcome measures is associated with improved treatment effectiveness, treatment efficiency, and collaborative practice.

**Brattland et al. (2018)** ran a randomized controlled trial showing that routine outcome monitoring improved therapy outcomes during an implementation process.

**Swift & Greenberg (2012)** published a meta-analysis on premature discontinuation (drop-out) in psychotherapy, finding that systematic attention to the therapeutic relationship reduces early termination.

**Graßmann et al. (2020)** conducted a meta-analysis demonstrating that the working alliance between service provider and client relates significantly to client outcomes.

This research comes from clinical settings, but the core finding translates: when staff pay attention to how participants are responding and adjust their approach, people stay engaged and do better.

## Where This Framework Comes From

This framework emerged from three major initiatives:

- **Ontario Trillium Foundation's Outcome Measurement Platform** (2020–2024) — Working with hundreds of OTF-funded organizations to design data collection tools, processes, and reports
- **Financial empowerment services projects** with West Neighbourhood House and Prosper Canada — Developing case management approaches for programs serving diverse clients
- **A four-year evaluation** (2019–2023) of services for newcomer and racialized women experiencing intimate partner violence, involving five community agencies in the Greater Toronto Area

These projects confirmed patterns across evaluations: limited staff time for evaluation activities, data collection seen as low-value burden, tools not integrated into regular work, and final reports arriving when everyone is too exhausted to implement changes.

The framework addresses these problems directly.
