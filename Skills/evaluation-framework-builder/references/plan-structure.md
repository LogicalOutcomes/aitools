# Evaluation plan structure

A complete plan has these 10 sections, in this order. Don't add, remove, or reorder sections. Use sentence case for headings. When input is limited, keep the full structure and use placeholder language (see SKILL.md) rather than dropping sections.

**Two allowances (see SKILL.md):**

- **Right-sizing.** All 10 sections are always present, but their *depth and cadence* may be scaled to the organization's capacity. For a very small program (e.g., ≤2 FTE), Section 7 may assign one person to several roles, Section 9 may collapse the six meetings to the two or three that matter, and Section 8 may lighten the reporting cadence. Sections 7–10 are otherwise as important as 1–6 — do not truncate or summarize them.
- **Add-on modules** fold into the existing sections (a module shows up in Objectives, Questions, the Framework, and the Schedule) — they do **not** add new top-level sections.

## 1. Program overview

Describe the program from research and the user's description: what it does, who it serves, how participants move through it. Include:

- A target population table — caption "Target population", columns `Characteristic` | `Details` (rows: age range, eligibility, geography, delivery mode)
- A numbered list of 6–10 key activities, each bolded with a one-sentence description

## 2. Evaluation objectives

State that the evaluation will increase the program's capacity to:

1. Meet participant needs and help participants achieve their goals
2. Achieve program goals and outcomes related to the program's specific aims
3. Improve program quality and fidelity to the program model
4. Increase responsiveness to participants in how services are delivered
5. Increase accessibility and equity in service provision
6. Increase responsiveness to key interest groups

Customize every objective for this specific program — each must name actual program goals, populations, or service elements, never generic evaluation language. Format as bold numbered objectives, each followed by a customizing sentence. Standalone section — never merge with evaluation questions.

## 3. Logic model

Two separate 3-column tables — never one combined 6-column table:

- **Table 1: Program resources and activities** — `Inputs` | `Activities` | `Outputs`
- **Table 2: Expected outcomes** — `Short-term (0–3 months)` | `Mid-term (3–12 months)` | `Long-term (future goals)`

(The handbook draws this as a single six-column model. LogicalOutcomes splits it into two 3-column tables so it stays readable and meets WCAG reflow on narrow screens and in print; the content is identical.)

Include a note that long-term outcomes are aspirational and not directly measured — progress shows in mid-term data patterns over 2+ years.

## 4. Evaluation questions

The 8 standard questions, each customized: after the question, add "Specifically:" with program-specific detail.

1. What activities are provided by the program?
2. To what extent is the program being implemented as designed and meeting quality standards?
3. What are the characteristics of the populations served?
4. What do participants think about services and what are their suggestions?
5. What do staff and other interest groups think about services and what are their suggestions?
6. To what extent are participants meeting their goals?
7. What short-term and mid-term changes are participants experiencing?
8. What evidence suggests the program is contributing to its desired long-term impact?

## 5. Interest groups analysis

One table, columns: `Interest Group` | `What They Hope For` | `Risks From Their Perspective` | `Engagement Approach` | `How Input Shapes Services`. Include 3–6 rows relevant to the program (participants, staff, funders, employer/community partners, families...). The last column is the point of the section — it must state concretely how each group's feedback changes the program.

## 6. Evaluation framework

8–11 indicators total, at least 2 per category, each category in its own 4-column table (`Outputs/Outcomes` | `Indicators` | `Data Source/Method` | `Frequency`):

- **Outputs** (3–5) — what the program produces, including program fidelity (did staff deliver it correctly?)
- **Short-term outcomes** (3–5) — immediate participant changes (0–3 months), including participant experience
- **Mid-term outcomes** (2–4) — program goals achieved 3–12 months after participation
- **Long-term outcomes (aspirational)** — not directly measured; inferred from mid-term data over 2+ years

**Match indicators to the tracker they actually have** (see SKILL.md, "Working with the tracker they actually have"). Don't tie an indicator to a tracker field the organization doesn't keep. Where tracking is weak or partial, set the `Data Source/Method` to small-sample interviews and document review to *estimate* the outcome, rather than implying a field that doesn't exist. Where there is no tracker at all, limit this section to outputs and process measures plus small-sample interviews, and use the Schedule to start a minimal tracker first.

Include the continuous improvement cycle: collect feedback → review in monthly staff meetings → discuss with decision-makers → implement changes → document and repeat. State that each monthly staff meeting should produce at least one documented service improvement or issue for investigation.

**Equity disaggregation.** Where the data supports it, note in the relevant `Data Source/Method` cells that outputs and outcomes will be broken down by population group (e.g., demographics, language, geography) to check whether the program works differently for different groups, and that intakes and drop-outs will be compared against the target population. Only disaggregate data that can carry it: tracker fields collected with consent, or interviews/surveys whose response rate and design support the breakdown. Don't over-segment small or self-selected samples, and don't report group cells small enough to identify an individual.

## 7. Project roles

Open by noting that evaluation is a project — without clear ownership it becomes nobody's priority. Table captioned "Assigned roles", columns `Role` | `Responsibilities`, covering these four roles:

- **Sponsor** — champions evaluation at leadership level; ensures staff time and resources; approves reports; removes barriers
- **Liaison** — primary internal contact; manages scheduling; coordinates between evaluation and program delivery
- **Project Owner** — accountable for evaluation approach and quality; validates findings; conducts interviews; prepares annual reports
- **Project Manager** — day-to-day coordination; maintains tracker data quality; monitors review schedule; prepares feedback summaries

All four roles must be filled, but **in a small organization one person may hold more than one** (commonly Liaison + Project Owner) — name the combination explicitly rather than dropping a role. Add a note on which actual program staff could fill each role, and describe how remaining program staff participate: monthly feedback reviews, implementing improvements, contributing observations to the tracker.

## 8. Schedule

Four phases with specific activities in each:

- **Phase 1: Planning and initiation** — review and customize this plan, engage interest groups, enhance the participant tracker with recommended fields (or start a minimal one if none exists), establish baseline data, assign the Project Manager role
- **Phase 2: Design and development** — develop the interview protocol for small-sample interviews, create standard reports, train staff on the continuous improvement cycle, conduct first interviews, hold the first monthly review meeting
- **Phase 3: Implementation (ongoing)** — collect feedback during service delivery, conduct interviews at planned intervals, monthly staff data reviews, quarterly interest group check-ins, implement documented improvements
- **Phase 4: Analysis and reporting (quarterly + annual)** — quarterly output reports and dashboards, annual comprehensive report, multi-year trend analysis after 2+ years

Include a reporting schedule table — caption "Reporting schedule", columns `Report` | `Frequency` | `Audience` | `Content` — covering monthly feedback highlights, quarterly progress reports, and the annual comprehensive evaluation. For a very small program, a lighter cadence (e.g., monthly review + a single annual report) is acceptable — match it to capacity.

## 9. Meeting agendas

Table captioned "Meeting agendas", columns `Meeting Purpose` | `Deliverables` | `Minimum Attendees` | `Notes`, with these six meetings:

1. Initiate the project — customize this plan
2. Define objectives and interest groups — approve the plan with confirmed measures
3. Configure tracker and data tools — enhance tracker, create interview protocols, set up reports
4. Track progress — monthly data review and service improvements
5. Engage with data — quarterly trend analysis and program adjustments
6. Engage with recommendations — annual comprehensive report and strategic recommendations

List attendees by role title (matching Section 7). Each Notes cell needs 2–3 sentences of operational guidance, not a one-liner. For a very small program, these six may be collapsed to the two or three that matter most (typically: define objectives and roles; monthly data review; annual recommendations) — note which meetings have been combined rather than omitting the work.
